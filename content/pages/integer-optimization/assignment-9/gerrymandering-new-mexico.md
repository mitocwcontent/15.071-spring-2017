In every even-numbered year, the citizens who reside in each district can vote in an election to determine the <em>representative</em> of that district. The representative is a member of the House of Representatives, which is one of the two chambers of the Congress of the United States. Representatives hold great power, as they can propose and vote on bills that later can become laws.&nbsp;</p> <p>Each representative typically is affiliated with one of the two major political parties in the United States: the Democratic Party or the Republican Party. Each party naturally wants to ensure that they have as many representatives in Congress as possible. One way of achieving this is through <em>gerrymandering</em>.</p> <p>Gerrymandering refers to the process of redrawing district boundaries so as to favor a particular political party. To illustrate this, suppose we have the hypothetical state below, with three districts. Each district is further subdivided along a grid into smaller subregions, where each subregion votes unanimously for either party. Suppose that in this hypothetical example, there is only one voter in each subregion.</p> <p><img src="./resolveuid/2cc4ad885d2156d89c14e3848a5fb9dc" alt="Example of a gerrymandering grid in which Blue wins." /></p> <p>Based on the current district&nbsp;boundaries, the blue party has a majority in each district, so each district elects a blue representative. However, suppose we decide to redraw the boundaries in the following way:</p> <p><img src="./resolveuid/96516b5e431f1672ee8ca633d2d2e93e" alt="Example of the same gerrymandering grid in which red wins." /></p> <p>Now the blue party does not win in every district; in fact, the red party wins two of the three districts.&nbsp;</p> <p>In this problem, we will be exploring how to systematically manipulate these kinds of boundaries. We will be doing this specifically for the state of New Mexico, which is one of the fifty states of the United States.</p> <h2 class="subhead">The Data</h2> <p>The state of New Mexico, located in the south of the US, currently has three districts:</p> <p><img alt="Colored map showing New Mexico's congressional districts." src="./resolveuid/a716bfa776b2be85f666b0a4e6877f69" /></p> <p>The state of New Mexico is also divided into counties:</p> <p><img alt="Map showing the outlines of New Mexico's counties." src="./resolveuid/47316383de173f457bcc8726c7c9aef7" /></p> <p>Counties are administrative units that are typically smaller than districts. In many states counties are split across districts, but in this problem<span style="font-size: 1em; line-height: 1.6em;">&nbsp;we will assume that the new districts we will design will be built from the existing counties.&nbsp;</span></p> <p>We have the voting record from the 2012 presidential election for each county in New Mexico. We will use the presidential election voting record of each county in 2012 as a proxy for how the county will vote in the next election for the house of representatives. This data is provided in <span style="text-decoration: underline;"><a href="./resolveuid/e584a070e342e7b633663ac538a60f69">Gerrymandering (ODS)</a></span>&nbsp;for LibreOffice and OpenOffice, or <span style="text-decoration: underline;"><a href="./resolveuid/378b5f186924f2c3e9a0c48f4f366fef">Gerrymandering (XLSX)</a></span>&nbsp;for Excel.</p> <h2 class="subhead">The Problem</h2> <p>In the 2012 House of Representatives election, the Democratic party won in New Mexico&rsquo;s 1st and 3rd districts, while the Republican party won in the 2nd district.&nbsp;</p> <p>Suppose that we have the opportunity to gerrymander New Mexico, so that we still have three districts. Is there a way to redesign the three districts so that the Democratic party takes all three districts?&nbsp;</p> <p>This is the topic of our problem &mdash; and we will tackle it using integer optimization. Let&rsquo;s formulate this problem as follows.</p> <p>Our principal decision variables are \(x_{ij}\) defined for each district \(i\) and each county \(j\), where \(x_{ij}\) is 1 if county \(j\) is assigned to district \(i\) and 0 otherwise.&nbsp;</p> <p>The first scenario we will consider is selecting&nbsp;our objective to maximize the number of votes that the Democratic party wins district 2 by (remember that the Democratic party lost in the 2nd district). If we can get the Democratic party to win district 2 by a margin of at least 100 votes while still winning districts 1 and 3 by a margin of at least 100 votes in each district, the Democratic party will win all three districts.</p> <p>With regard to constraints, we would like:</p> <ul>     <li><span style="font-size: 1em; line-height: 1.6em;">Each county to be assigned to exactly one district;</span></li>     <li><span style="font-size: 1em; line-height: 1.6em;">Each district to consist of at least one county; and</span></li>     <li><span style="font-size: 1em; line-height: 1.6em;">The Democratic party to still win districts 1 and 3 by a margin of at least 100 votes.</span></li> </ul> <h2 class="subhead">Problem 1.1 -
